Does the Silicon Valley mindset give companies a pass for not having a business model because that would hinder innovation?

  
Brandon Smietana, Founder of Symbolic Analytics
3 votes by Matt Scullin, Alexander Dominic Horré and Ori Barbut
There are two types of companies.  There are web 2.0 companies that have lots of eye-balls (content) and there are companies with business models.

For instance, tech crunch sold for 40 million.

If you have a startup with 6000 subscribers at $30/month and you sell your company for 20x earnings.  That is 43 million dollars.

If you are ad supported and your eCPM rate is $0.05 cents (like Facebook/4-Chan/Dating sites), then you need 3.6 billion page views per month to hit 40 million valuation at 20x earnings.

Alternatively, you can find 60 companies that will pay you $3000 a month for a service (SaS) and have a 40 million valuation at 20x earnings.

The idea behind many of these Web 2.0 sites like Slide and Path is not to make money.  The idea is that Google and Microsoft have made offers for twitter that value the company at over $60 per active user.

The assets of the company are the employees, the platform and user-base; not the revenue stream of the company.

It would probably be more effective for Google to pay people $60 per active Buzz user they sign up, than to buy Twitter at $60/user, but they will try to buy Twitter anyways.  Yahoo made an offer to Facebook of $260 per user.  Will Facebook even be able to realize revenues of $260 per user over the lifetime of the user?

Companies will pay a premium to acquire platform control and to buy companies like Path to acquire their user-bases.  It is not very clear why they will pay a premium for these users if they have no effective way of monetizing the user-base.

Technology companies in general do not pay dividends and often have a cash hoard they need to get rid of.  Google has so much money that it is opening up a corporate bond trading desk to invest it.  Maybe a 300 million dollar acquisition of a company like Slide at $50 a user increases the market cap of companies like Google, more than the loss of cash decreases the market cap of these companies.

Historically, most mergers and acquisitions are futile and are a waste of money.  Sometimes they are strategic, but more often they are represent a net loss of value for the acquiring company, but companies keep doing these large value destroying acquisitions for unknown reasons.

People will continue to start companies with no business model and no expectations for revenue, as long as these companies continue to be bought.

Smaller, early stage acquisitions however can create value and are a valuable source of new products and technological innovations.  This is one reasons angel portfolios have been doing so well in recent years.  These companies usually exit below 30 million and the returns are too small to interest traditional VC funds, but they have been out-performing VC by a wide margin.

I think there is still however a whole cottage industry of building up companies with no revenue and flipping them (ex. Twitter).  Only a few very high profile investors with strong connections to the potential acquiring companies will be able to generate returns on these companies however.

Brandon Smietana, I write machine learning based s...
Do you want an index fund or the market portfolio?

If you want a market fund and you have a factor model or the CAPM is wrong then your market portfolio will be different than the index fund.

In general, it does not matter how much money you have.  You can achieve extremely low transaction costs from places like IB.  Tracking error does not affect your rate of return much.

In fact, your rate of return may be higher if you are investing in the market portfolio (as determined by mean-variance analysis and your factor model).  You will notice that in theory (CAPM) the market-fund and index fund are the same portfolio; however in practice they diverge significantly.

I would definitely just calculate the market fund and track that.  There are many advantages to doing it yourself, especially if you want to employ tax-loss harvesting strategies.

Facebook's P/E ratio is currently around 165, but it is incorrect to state that valuations represent discounted future profits.  Most technology companies do not pay dividends and investors prefer stock appreciation to dividends.  Studies show that the intangible assets of companies (such as organizational capital) account for much of the market capitalization of a company, beyond the value of the future discounted revenues of the company.

So for instance, a dollar invested internally in a company can increase its market cap more than if that dollar was realized as profits instead.

See: Wired for Innovation: How Information Technology is Reshaping the Economy http://amzn.to/aS5kzf

Facebook's valuation reflects the positioning of its technology platform and strategic options in entering adjacent markets, such as display advertising.

What is more interesting, is the question of why Facebook would accept additional investment at all.  These recent Facebook rounds seem unnecessary from a financial perspective.  To increase Facebook's valuation in the secondary market, while setting expectations for an IPO?  To reward insiders with a 10% yearly profit for holding shares until IPO?

What makes the gameplay, technology and monetization engines of each of the following games hard to make?  The games: Travian, Metin 2, Dark Orbit, Runescape, Runes of Magic and Maple Story.

  
Brandon Smietana, Founder of Symbolic Analytics
2 votes by Marius Comper and Seun Osewa
Nothing.

There are hundreds of micro-MMOs, many of which were built by single people.

The primary limitations are knowledge.

This is a very broad topic and I cannot cover it exhaustively, but will gloss over some of the things which will help you become more successful.

Marketing> 

Do you know how to drive user growth?  Viral marketing, affiliate marketing, word of mouth, and so on.  Runescape succeeded because it had an innovative affiliate relationship with one of the larger gaming studios.  World of Warcraft used advertisements on the Battlenet platform to drive WoW growth (free customer acquisition).

There were many similar RPGs to Runescape, but its affiliate marketing program set it apart.

Technical>

Do you know how to get around relational database performance limitations?  Can you solve the scalability issues?  How do you distribute patches to the client?  Do you know how to prevent item duplication?  Do you have one continuous world or do you need to shard the servers?  Do you know how to use compression to lower bandwidth costs?

Many successful MMOs died because they grew to the point that they were no longer playable, because they were hitting relational database performance limitations and were too poorly designed to easily modify.  If you have experience building scalability systems, you can build in application level database partitioning from day one.

In a recent engine we built, we abandoned a relational database all together and stored everything in ram (with the database only being used for serialization).  That improved performance from 2000 reads per second to over 85,000 reads per second.  That is a 40x savings in hardware costs.

We similarly achieved a 50x savings in bandwidth costs by adapting advanced compression methodologies (an arithmetic coding implementation tailored to the message we were sending).

Today any developer can throw up an MMO in flash and get 800k users on the Facebook platform.  However, to scale you need to have an intimate understanding of hardware performance, software design for distributed high performance systems, compression algorithms and computer networking.

Even extremely successful companies such as the makers of Everyquest lacked some of these organizational capacities.  Everquest began to save 3 million dollars a month when they realized they could compress client/server communications!

Monetization>

Do you know how to optimize your monetization strategies?  Are you going to monetize on subscriptions, in-game sales or content packs?  Should your game be freemium or subscription?

Very large companies with extremely competent and experienced industry executives often make horrible errors of judgement when making these choices.  One MMO for instance, began as a subscription based service and was unprofitable, but later switched to free to play and began to grow quickly and eventually became a cash-cow.

Monetization requires experience with A/B testing.  Most companies do not have experience with analytics and this depresses their revenue growth.

Companies like Zynga, who have mastered A/B testing methodologies are raping everyone else in the industry.  Consider how simple Farmville is compared to your average MMO RPG which requires 50 developers and 300 artists working 4 years to produce.  Consider that Farmville is also more profitable than most MMO RPGs.

New platforms such as Facebook have completely changed the revenue model of the flash gaming industry.  The economics of MMOs are also being changed by new revenue models inspired by the success of games on the Facebook platform.

The economics of game production are also changing rapidly.  Today a 2 person development team can push out an MMO RPG on Facebook in 3 months of development time and get 60,000 users and $30,000/month in revenue.  

Compare this to the 100 million dollar, 4 year, 250 employee development projects typical of traditional MMOs.  Today's MMOs can break even at a much larger scale.  You wont be buying a Gulfstream with your social gaming revenue, but a 300k MAU game is Ferrari money.

The proliferation of MMOs and their decreasing cost is causing increasing market segmentation.

The scale at which you can break even is much smaller and the number of MMOs is increasing every year.  The challenge is no longer building the MMO, but acquiring users, retaining users and monetizing users.

Today, thousands of innovative games and game-play mechanisms are produced each year, by innovative independent developers.  However, most of these games are commercial failures, not because they are not innovative or because they are not fun; but rather because their creators do not have skill in marketing and customer acquisition strategies.

For instance, games like Farmville (which are not very 'fun') can be extremely successful commercially with the right marketing strategies, and extensive A/B testing your monetization strategy, viral marketing, user retention and conversion funnels.

Also, cross promotion between your games is becoming a much more important marketing strategy.  Companies like Zynga leverage their existing user-base to lower their customer acquisition cost for their new games.

Going into the future, I think we will see many larger studios which are organized as several independent development teams within a larger company, where the marketing, A/B testing and technical back-end architecture is shared between the development groups.

We are also seeing groups of independent studios forming larger aggregate cross promoting groups.  This may put many of the medium sized studios on equal footing with companies like Zynga who are able to leverage their large installed base to lower their customer acquisition costs.

Companies like Google can afford to hire post-modern philosophy Phd's for secretary positions and can hire comp sci. Phds from top schools to write Javascript.  Unless you are Google, the job should be matched to the skills people have.

Most things are routine and the people do not have to be especially good programmers to implement them.  I do not hire AI or machine learning Phds  for 150k a year to write CSS.

The reality is that most companies do not have enough design work for a full time designer.

I would rather have 5 designer/programmers who were ok, than 4 engineers and a full-time designer.  Good design comes from trying a large number of possible designs and choosing the best one (that is how Apple and many of the best designers do it).

You might need some good engineers for some things, but expecting your employees to be 25x at everything is unrealistic and will impair the performance of your company.

If a person designs without regard to the implementation (design perspective), the implementation may be inelegant and difficult/expensive to maintain and modify.

If a person designs with only the implementation in mind, but not how people will interact with the data (engineering prospective) the design may be maintainable and separated into independent components, but may not be as usable.

When designing applications and workflow, you are always making trade-offs between human usability and ease of implementation/maintainability.

A designer+engineer is best able to make those trade-offs.

Where is human technology ultimately headed? What meaning lies at the essence of the human endeavor to develop technology?

  
Brandon Smietana, Founder of Symbolic Analytics

The human body, the human mind are a prison.  All technology exists to liberate us from that prison.

From automobiles, to jets and cellular phones; technology augments human capacities beyond the endowments of nature.

Most importantly however, technology is a tool of power.  Individuals do not control technology for their ends (with a few recent exceptions), but rather technology is developed and employed by groups or associations of people laboring to accomplish the goals of dominant socio-cultural elites.

Technology both increases the span of control of the individual and the small group and is a tool employed by existing powerful institutions (government, advertising agencies, defense industry, corporations) to increase their power and operational capacities.

Technology is not a democratic institution and if individuals find liberation in it, that is an unintended side effect which will be addressed through political economy and the apparatus of the state.

I think the next century will be characterized by the violent conflict between the applications of technology controlled by democratic institutions and the need for existing institutions to co-opt and control these alternative governance structures.

I recently learned that the founders of my successful early-stage startup are involved in very suspect and possibly illegal business practices impacting high-profile clients. How can I report this without jeopardizing my own prospects in the very tight-knit start-up world?

  
Brandon Smietana, Founder of Symbolic Analytics
3 votes by Anthony Foster, J.C. Hewitt and John Selogy
You should consult a lawyer and carefully evaluate the pros and cons of whistle-blowing before taking action.

You should investigate the company's behavior and consult a lawyer.  Many corrupt or questionable business practices are shady, but not quite illegal, while many reasonable business practices are illegal (AirBNB, UberTaxi, early Paypal operations).

If you choose to go forward with whistle-blowing, you have to understand the possible consequences and ways your employer may retaliate against you.  You need to take measures to insulate yourself from blow-back from your whistle blowing actions.  If possible, you should remove yourself from the firm in advance of the whistle-blowing.

You should also keep in mind that by reporting business practices, you will likely violate the trust others have placed in you.  You may lose friends and valuable business contacts.  You may be the subject of rumors and character assassination both in the media and in the business communities you frequent.

It is likely you will be ostracized by your friends and employers if you take action and if your whistle blowing activities become known.  If you are an executive, you will likely no longer be considered "trustworthy" by many people and may find that your employment options are more limited than before.

History has shown that the whistle blowers in white collar crime are punished more severely than the executives who violate the law.  They lose their jobs and their reputation.  They are ostracized and undergo systematic character assassination, while the executives involved keep their jobs, the government fines their company and business proceeds as usual.

In the Lysine Price Fixing Conspiracy, the largest price fixing conspiracy in human history that has ever been prosecuted, the executives involved in the price fixing were charged with three years imprisonment and were let off on probation while the whistle blower, Mark Whitacre, spent 8.5 years in prison without chance of probation.

Mark Whitacre was charged with embezzlement in retaliation for whistle blowing  as part of the company's character assassination tactics.

You should consult a lawyer and determine if you are likely to be prosecuted for reporting the business practice.  If you report information about business practices which are not illegal, but which damage the company's reputation, then you can expect legal retaliation which may cost hundreds of thousands of dollars to defend against.

The company executives will receive lawyers on the company's tab to defend them (probably paid for by B&O insurance), while you will be fired and have to pay your own legal defense out of pocket.

If you report the incident to the media, you may be subject to libel and other lawsuits in retaliation by the company.  You should carefully consider how your response will subject you to legal liability and possible retaliation.

Possible and commonly used methods of retaliation and character assassination includind "finding" pornography (and even child pornography) on your work company, finding you engaged in accounting frauds and claims of sexual harassment or drug abuse.  Systematically leaking incriminating rumors to the media shortly after learning about your insubordination is common.  Your company is likely to dominate the media discourse around the disclosure.

Companies have pressured and succeeded at getting DAs to charge whistle-blowing employees with Felony "unauthorized computer access" charges for using Facebook or personal email accounts at work!  The most politically connected and powerful your company is, the more dangerous whistle-blowing attempts are.  Large companies will hire computer forensics experts to weed out whistle-blowers and often employ extremely aggressive legal tactics.

You may be frustrated that character assassination pieces about your conduct are given more media attention then the fraudulent business practices that you were trying to bring to light.

Everything you did at the company will be scrutinized.  Did you make a small clerical error on an expense report any-time in the past 15 years?  The company may report that you were terminated for "Falsifying expense reports", even if they were prepared by your administrative assistant.  Did you have a relationship with any subordinates or coworkers?  They will be pressured and financially incentiviced to bring forth charges and allegations, aiding in the character assassination.

Unfounded and vague sexual harassment charges charges and other forms of character assassination may result in pressure for 3rd parties on your wife to seek a divorce.  Friends and family will not know your story unless you tell them, they will only know what the media tells them about what happened.  Do not be surprised if the larger company with better media relations, dominates the disclosure.

Be prepared for media spin and be prepared to defend your reputation publicly.  You may want to hire a PR firm.

You should also keep in mind, that except in cases where the government was defrauded, you will not benefit financially in any way from reporting this possibly fraudulent practice.  At best, the most likely outcome is that you are fired in retaliation, you violate the trust of the business community and the company is fined a token amount and continues to operate as usual.

At worse, you are fired in retaliation, lose the trust of the business community, you experience character assassination (they have a larger PR budget and more clout than you do) and  the company's lawyers get the legal threat buried through a token settlement or clever legal arguments.

The most politically adroit thing to do does not involve going on a moral crusade which may cost you your job, reputation, hundreds of thousands of dollars in legal defense (against the prosecutors and against retaliatory lawsuits against you) and which does not benefit you financially.

If you are uncomfortable with your company's business practices, then the best action is usually walking away.

If you express your moral or ethical concern about the legality of the company's business practices, you may even be able to negotiate a generous severance package while keeping your reputation intact and putting the company on notice about the practices.  Executives cannot claim plausible deniability after being notified of the practices.

Whistle-blowing is one area where "doing what is right" may not also be in your best interest.

I want to tell you "Do what is right! Ya, you will feel better about it!", however the reality is that it is not worth having your life destroyed by character assassination and legal fees.  People have been fired, assassinated in the media and driven into bankruptcy, divorce and suicide because they "did what is right".

The US law simply does not protect whistle-blowers.  You need to consult a lawyer and carefully weigh the advantages of whistle-blowing against the overwhelming disadvantages.

This advice is not "politically correct", but it is what most low-profile whistle blowers will tell you.  Its generally not worth it.  You should avoid taking credit for whistle-blowing except in very high-profile cases.  You should collect evidence, leave your position and attempt to provide information to the media anonymously.  You should test the media's reaction to the information (most whistle-blowing attempts are ignored by the media or covered up).

If you decide to come forward publicly, it should only be after the whistle-blowing attempt has acquired public support and you are no longer at risk of character assassination.  People overestimate how receptive the media will be to their whistle-blowing attempts.  The media is more interested in corporate executives having affairs with b-movie stars and baseless sexual harassment allegation than it is about embezzlement and routine white collar crime.

In the overwhelming majority of cases, it just best to walk away.

    Does the Silicon Valley mindset give companies a pass for not having a business model because that would hinder innovation?

    There are two types of companies.  There are web 2.0 companies that have lots of eye-balls (content) and there are companies with business models. (more)
    There are two types of companies.  There are web 2.0 companies that have lots of eye-balls (content) and there are companies with business models.

    For instance, tech crunch sold for 40 million.

    If you have a startup with 6000 subscribers at $30/month and you sell your company for 20x earnings.  That is 43 million dollars.

    If you are ad supported and your eCPM rate is $0.05 cents (like Facebook/4-Chan/Dating sites), then you need 3.6 billion page views per month to hit 40 million valuation at 20x earnings.

    Alternatively, you can find 60 companies that will pay you $3000 a month for a service (SaS) and have a 40 million valuation at 20x earnings.

    The idea behind many of these Web 2.0 sites like Slide and Path is not to make money.  The idea is that Google and Microsoft have made offers for twitter that value the company at over $60 per active user.

    The assets of the company are the employees, the platform and user-base; not the revenue stream of the company.

    It would probably be more effective for Google to pay people $60 per active Buzz user they sign up, than to buy Twitter at $60/user, but they will try to buy Twitter anyways.  Yahoo made an offer to Facebook of $260 per user.  Will Facebook even be able to realize revenues of $260 per user over the lifetime of the user?

    Companies will pay a premium to acquire platform control and to buy companies like Path to acquire their user-bases.  It is not very clear why they will pay a premium for these users if they have no effective way of monetizing the user-base.

    Technology companies in general do not pay dividends and often have a cash hoard they need to get rid of.  Google has so much money that it is opening up a corporate bond trading desk to invest it.  Maybe a 300 million dollar acquisition of a company like Slide at $50 a user increases the market cap of companies like Google, more than the loss of cash decreases the market cap of these companies.

    Historically, most mergers and acquisitions are futile and are a waste of money.  Sometimes they are strategic, but more often they are represent a net loss of value for the acquiring company, but companies keep doing these large value destroying acquisitions for unknown reasons.

    People will continue to start companies with no business model and no expectations for revenue, as long as these companies continue to be bought.

    Smaller, early stage acquisitions however can create value and are a valuable source of new products and technological innovations.  This is one reasons angel portfolios have been doing so well in recent years.  These companies usually exit below 30 million and the returns are too small to interest traditional VC funds, but they have been out-performing VC by a wide margin.

    I think there is still however a whole cottage industry of building up companies with no revenue and flipping them (ex. Twitter).  Only a few very high profile investors with strong connections to the potential acquiring companies will be able to generate returns on these companies however.
